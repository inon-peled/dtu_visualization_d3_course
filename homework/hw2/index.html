Part 1: Telling Data Stories

The questions below refer to article Narrative Visualization: Telling Stories with Data , by Edward Segel and Jeffrey Heer.

  1. What is the Oxford English Dictionary's definition of a narrative?

    The Oxford English Dictionary's definition of a narrative is: "an account of a series of events, facts, etc., given in order and with the establishing of connections between them."

  2. What is your favorite visualization among the examples in section 3? Explain why in a few words.

    Our favorite visualization in section 3 is "Fig. 1: Steroids Or Not, the Pursuit is On. New York Times." We like it because it is clean, uses few colors aesthetically and consistently, guides the visual flow well, and effectively delivers the main findings in the data.

  3. What's the point of Figure 7?

    Figure 7 provides a summary of the research findings, namely the design space of narrative visualization -- its components (in columns) distributed over the sample data (in rows) -- along with noticeable patterns. Patterns of consistent utilization are seen as dark blue regions, whereas patterns of under-utilization are seen as light blue regions.

  5. Check out Figure 8 and section 4.3: What is your favorite genre of narrative visualization? Why?

    Our favorite genre of narrative visualization is Partitioned Poster, because it combines multiple levels of detail in an accessible format: a main image captures the attention of the viewer, who can then choose which of the smaller images and text to read.

  6. Check out Figure 8 and section 4.3: What is your least favorite genre? Why?

    Our least favorite genre of narrative visualization is Flow Chart, because it is a dry format, which often feels more like a technical specification rather than an interesting data story.
