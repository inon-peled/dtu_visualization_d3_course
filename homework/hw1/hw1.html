Part 1: DAOST Chapter 2 Questions

  1. Q: Explain in your own words the point of the jitter plot.

    A: The point of the jitter box is to visually distinguish between overlapping points in one dimensional data, while keeping points close to their original locations.

  2. Q: Explain in your own words the point of figure 2-3. (I'm going to skip saying \"in your own words\" going forward, but I hope you get the point; I expect all answers to be in your own words)

    A: The point of figure 2-3 is to show a disadvantage of histograms, namely their sensitivity to placement of bin edges, such that the same data appears very differently distributed for different choices of anchor points. This also means that there is generally no one-to-one correspondence between histogram and data.

  3. Q: The author of DAOST (Philipp Janert) likes KDEs (and think they're better than histograms). And I don't. I didn't give a detailed explanation in the video, but now that works to my advantage. I'll ask you guys to think about this and thereby create an excellent exercise: When can KDEs be misleading?

    A: KDE can be misleading when e.g. visualizing few samples from a uniform distribution. If the samples are spaced widely, the resulting KDE will look like a sequence of hills, whereas the actual distribution is a horizontal interval. Conversely, a histogram with few bins will faithfully represent the distribution.

  4. Q: I've discussed some strengths of the CDF - there are also weaknesses. Janert writes "[CDFs] have less intuitive appeal than histograms or KDEs". What does he mean by that?

    A: Because a CDF is cumulative, it does not immediately convey the shape of the density function, but rather allow to quickly answer questions about totals. In contrast, histograms and KDE suggest the form of the underlying density function.

  5. Q: What is a Quantile plot? What is it good for?

    A: A quantile plot is a graph of the CDF with the x and y axes interchanged. Thus a quantile plot conveys the same information as CDF, but allows more easily to derive any quantile, i.e. the smallest datum which is greater than X% of the data.

  6. Q: How is a Probablity Plot defined? What is it useful for? Have you ever seen one before?

    A: A probability plot is a method for showing how similar a data set is to another distribution Φ with mean μ and standard deviation σ. It is a plot of the data set values xi as a function of Φâˆ’1(yi), where yi is the quantile of xi. Φ then corresponds to a straight line with slope σ and zero intercept μ, while dissimilarities with the data set are seen as deviations from the line. I have seen probability plots before, yay.

  7. Q: One of the reasons I like DAOST is that Janert is so suspicious of mean, median, and related summary statistics. Explain why one has to be careful when using those - and why visualization of the full data is always better.

    A: Data sets with very different underlying distributions may have the same summary statistics, hence summary statistics are not sufficient information about distributions. An acute example of this is Anscombe's Quartet. Therefore, to convey the main properties of a distribution, visualization of the full data must accompany the summary statistics.

  8. Q: I love box plots. When are box plots most useful?

    A: Box plots compactly present summary statistics, main quantiles, and outliers. The boxes and outliers are drawn in proprtion to the width of the distribution at hand, hence box plots are useful for comparing the main structural properties of several data sets: widths, location, and frequency of outliers.

  9. Q: The book doesn't mention violin plots. Are those better or worse than box plots? Why?

    A: Compared to box plots, violin plots add more information, such as shape of data density. Neither method is better or worse, rather each may be more appropriate depending on what properties we wish to visually convey. If we wish to convey quantile structure and outliers, box plots are more suitable, whereas if we wish to convey overall density shape, violin plots are more suitable.

This is Quantifiably the Best Month to go to the Farmers Market

As part of a homework assignment, I ask my students at Pratt to go out and chart something of interest to them, and Katherine Savarese came back with a simple chart about farmers markets that I loved -- it inspired this post.

You probably know that farmers markets are a staple across all five boroughs of New York City, but September happens to be a very special month. Why is that? Well, it turns out September is the month with the most unique types of fresh produce -- forty three to be exact.

To show the current offerings, we charted how many types of fruits and vegetables are available by month, and showed if they were fresh or from storage.

If you miss the month of September, you might be behind the fruit curve, but there are plenty more months of vegetables left. The chart shows that fresh vegetables are available 9 out of 12 months, but fruit is only available 5 out of 12. Now if only we could quantify how delicious the produce is...
